\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction-en}

This chapter introduces asynchronous academic discussion facilitation and explains why sustained instructor presence becomes difficult under high participation or many simultaneous threads. It then defines the scope, objectives, research questions, work plan, and document structure.

\section{Context}

According to social constructivism, learning happens through interaction, in which students construct knowledge by negotiating meaning with others \parencite{WooReeves2007}. Debates are one of the techniques that best exploits this dynamic, because they increase critical thinking and collaborative learning in higher education \parencite{Brown2015}, give voice to quieter students and create a more equitable space for participation than the lecture format \parencite{BineyEkeyi2018,WooReeves2007}. In higher education, this type of discussion can take place in the classroom, where an instructor guides the exchange in real time, or outside it, through digital platforms. In this second case, the work is asynchronous, meaning participants contribute at different moments, from any location, without needing to coincide in the same space and time.

The digital platforms that make this asynchronous work possible have grown significantly in Europe in recent years. In 2021, 100\% of institutions in the European Higher Education Area already used some form of digital learning \parencite{GaebelEtAl2021}. By 2025, 75\% of universities use blended learning as their main modality and 36\% offer at least one fully online degree program \parencite{GaebelEtAl2025}. Depending on the type of institution, these platforms, known as learning management systems (LMS), are either the main modality of the course or a complement to in-person education. Solutions such as Moodle, Canvas, Open edX, or Coursera allow content, activities, and discussion forums to be organized in a single environment, and can be instructor-led or self-paced.

The 2020 pandemic accelerated this process significantly, and several institutions reported more progress in digitalization during the first four months of the crisis than in the four previous years \parencite{GaebelEtAl2021}. As a consequence, interaction between students came to happen almost exclusively through digital tools, and discussion forums became the main mechanism for that interaction in online education \parencite{Rovai2007,MasseyEtAl2019}.

Discussion forums in educational platforms offer forms of participation that the in-person classroom makes difficult, such as scale, turn-taking equity, and scheduling flexibility. They allow participation to scale to a number of students that would be unmanageable in a traditional classroom, as shown by a study with more than 5,000 students in parallel discussions through Moodle \parencite{ZulfikarEtAl2019}. Discussion forums also give each student more opportunities to participate than the in-person format, where the instructor can take up to 80\% of talk time \parencite{SullivanPratt1996}, while in forums everyone can contribute regardless of location or schedule availability \parencite{MasseyEtAl2019}. Finally, they enable new forms of assessment, such as rubrics based on the quality of contributions, which make it possible to measure critical thinking more directly than a traditional exam \parencite{HoSwan2007,Rovai2007}.

\section{Motivation}

The facilitation of asynchronous discussions falls mainly on the instructor, who performs organizational, intellectual, and social functions \parencite{Abdous2011}. Active facilitation is associated with more equitable participation \parencite{AnEtAl2009}, deeper critical thinking \parencite{LimEtAl2011}, and greater knowledge construction when roles are structured \parencite{DeWeverEtAl2010}. Sustaining this presence becomes difficult when participation volume or the number of simultaneous threads grows. Studies report that much instructor activity is social rather than instructional \parencite{RichardsonEtAl2015}, that corrective feedback can represent a small share of posts \parencite{BlignautTrollip2003}, and that facilitators struggle to preserve depth across concurrent discussions \parencite{Mansour2024}. These conditions intensify in massive courses \parencite{GuEtAl2021,YuEtAl2024maic}.

In recent years, platforms such as Coursera have integrated assistants based on large language models (LLMs) in more than 100 courses to answer student questions, with response times 47\% faster than human tutors \parencite{Coursera2024}, and edX reached 1.2 million students in six months with AI-powered virtual labs \parencite{MarketGrowthReports2026}. Available reviews document that these applications concentrate mainly on personalized tutoring, feedback generation, and curriculum design assistance \parencite{ChuEtAl2025}. However, the facilitation of group discussions remains less explored. Existing natural language processing (NLP) work focuses on classification (detecting toxicity, analyzing stance) rather than producing interventions that actively improve discussion quality \parencite{KorreEtAl2025}, and around 70\% of studies position AI as an environment that structures learning conditions, not as an agent that actively facilitates \parencite{CasebourneEtAl2025}. Some work addresses discussion facilitation directly, including a case-based reasoning system for generating suggestions in online forums \parencite{GuEtAl2021}, and MAIC (\textit{Multi-Agent Intelligent Classroom}), which scales the instructor figure in massive environments using multiple LLM agents \parencite{YuEtAl2024maic}.

\section{Scope}

This thesis focuses on asynchronous academic discussions organized as text threads, independently of the application that hosts them. They may come from discussion engines embedded in LMSs such as Open edX, Moodle, or Canvas, or from conversation platforms such as Discourse. The object of study is the opening post, replies, authors, and timestamps, rather than a particular platform.

Asynchronicity also bounds the technical problem. The system does not need to respond while a student is writing or within a real-time conversation; it can analyze a thread and prepare an intervention in the background. This decision reduces latency and concurrency requirements and allows the work to focus on the facilitation process: what information to observe, when to intervene, and what kind of response to propose. A reasonable execution time is therefore understood in this context, not as the response time of a synchronous conversational application.

The artifact receives threads through a common input contract, analyzes their state, and decides whether to generate an intervention. Its scope includes classification, selection of a facilitation role and technique, response generation, and traceable experiment records. Automatic activation and seamless publication inside each source platform were not generalized. Open edX was used to build and test one data source and a partial writing route; it is not an end-to-end instructor workflow.

The work partially follows the \textit{Design Science Research} process of \textcite{PeffersEtAl2007}: it identifies the problem, defines objectives, builds the artifact, demonstrates it with reproducible sources and a partial Open edX connection, and performs a preliminary technical evaluation. Educational utility with students was not evaluated.

\section{Objectives}

The objective is to design and implement an LLM-based pipeline that analyzes asynchronous academic discussions and produces traceable facilitation candidates independently of their source.

The specific objectives are the following:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Design and implement an LLM-based agent system, organized in stages, capable of producing interventions coherent with the discussion topic and with conversational continuity, within reasonable time and resource consumption.
  \item Define and implement a context engineering strategy grounded in the literature, integrating course content and pedagogical literature on discussions, with performance criteria derived from how an instructor effectively facilitates a discussion.
  \item Define a source-neutral input contract and test it with reproducible scenarios, six anonymized historical threads preserved in the repository, and a partial Open edX adapter.
  \item Evaluate the system's behavior in scenarios generated from prior studies, measuring the accuracy of conversation-state classification and the coherence of the generated interventions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Research questions}

The research began with exploratory questions about the problem, before any design decision was made. The starting point was the observation that asynchronous discussions in educational platforms do not produce the type of learning that does occur in an in-person classroom, and that it was not clear why, nor what could be done about it.

\subsection{Exploratory phase}

The first questions aimed to understand that dynamic:

\begin{itemize}
  \item What happens in an in-person classroom that does not happen in an asynchronous discussion? Why doesn't the social dynamic of learning carry over to text forums?
  \item What makes a facilitator effective? What concrete actions does an instructor produce when intervening in a discussion?
  \item What does the intervention process look like from the inside? What information does a facilitator need to make a decision?
  \item Is it possible to make that process observable and controllable, or is it opaque by nature?
\end{itemize}

These questions guided the literature review and the initial technical exploration. The answer to the fourth question, in particular, determined the direction of the design, since if the intervention process can be described as a set of observable conditions plus a corresponding action, it can be implemented as a pipeline; if it cannot, automation is not viable. Evidence from intelligent tutoring systems \parencite{VanLehn2011} and the literature on facilitation roles \parencite{PilkingtonWalker2003} suggested that it is possible, within limits.

\subsection{Design phase}

Once the problem was delimited, the questions became more concrete:

\begin{itemize}
  \item How can facilitation roles be operationalized as LLM agents in a discussion analysis pipeline?
  \item What criteria allow automatic detection of a discussion's state to determine whether and when to intervene?
  \item What criteria allow evaluation of whether an AI-generated intervention is pedagogically appropriate?
  \item Can discussions be received from different sources without coupling the pipeline to one platform?
\end{itemize}

These questions structure the development chapter, since each one corresponds to a system component or a documented design decision.

\section{Workflow}

The project uses an R\&D workflow inspired by agile practices, with kanban as the tracking tool, but without fixed sprints, because research uncertainty does not fit well with iterations of predetermined length, since some questions are resolved in days while others require weeks of exploration before a decision can be made.

In practice, the work is organized around discovery cycles, where each phase produces findings that inform the next, with GitHub issues, milestones, and Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) as the main tools. \textbf{Issues} capture what needs to be explored or implemented, with enough context to resume the work after some time. \textbf{ADRs} record every significant design decision, its reasoning, and the discarded alternatives, and function as the project's memory. \textbf{Milestones} delimit phases, and a phase closes when the open questions that define it have been answered, not when a deadline expires.

This thesis reports only design decisions backed by traceable evidence in results, tables, figures, or project ADRs. Before committing to an implementation, each area of technical uncertainty is addressed through a spike or proof of concept, without production quality, whose goal is to reduce uncertainty and document findings, not to deliver maintainable code.

\subsection{Use of AI assistants in the implementation}

The technical implementation was carried out with support from LLM-based programming assistants, in particular Claude and Codex. They were used to draft code and documentation, explore alternatives, locate inconsistencies, and propose tests. The work followed an iterative cycle of proposal, review, testing, and correction.

I defined the problem, accepted or rejected proposals, and remained responsible for decisions, experiments, and the final text. I did not calculate a percentage of human- versus AI-authored code because the changes passed through several iterations and Git history cannot reliably attribute every final line.
